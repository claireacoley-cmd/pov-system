Why this exists

I had five tools but no home for a clear point of view.

Notion held old stories. Pinterest was for visual inspiration. Drive had my old frameworks and bits of writing that never made it online. And then there were the LLMs playing host to whatever topics were interesting that week.

Nothing was lost, but I didn't know how to find any of it.

You might have a version of this.

Sure, your tools are different but I bet you know the feeling. You sit down to write. You need the thing that proves your belief about differentiation. It's in a note nested against recipes for mushroom stroganoff, a family birthday and bullets prepping a performance review. It's half-baked. Another thing you were meant to come back to and flesh out, to add a story of "that time" this thing happened to you. You remember it was exciting… until it wasn't.

Everything's organised by where it came from or when you saved it. Nothing's organised by why it matters.

So you start from scratch. Again. Reconstructing from memory what you've already collected, because none of it matches how you think.

The question that changes this is a small one.

Instead of "what is this?" you ask: why do I want to save this? Is it something I believe — and would I go to war for it, or give a polite nod? Perhaps it's not a belief at all. Maybe it's a proof point, a story that shaped a bigger belief, or a moment that changed how you think.

I came to realise that a personal story and a case study can both be evidence for the same belief. But to get that far you have to see the pieces in the same place.

That's what the POV System does. Your beliefs sit at the centre. Proof connects to them: your stories, other people's stories, examples, patterns, data. Craft references sit alongside: the formats, structures, and turns of phrase you want to reach for when you're expressing yourself. When you sit down to write or refine a strategy, you pull from your own accumulated thinking. You see what you've already gathered. You build on it instead of starting over.

And there's a thinking inbox, because most ideas aren't as good as we think they are and arrive half-formed. You hear something on a podcast or scribble a note at 11pm. There's something in it that you know is important, but you don't quite know what it is yet. The inbox is where that gets resolved through sparring, helping the thinking happen before the filing — and when it's done, the idea sits alongside the evidence that got you there.

Beliefs come from proof and show up in the world via craft.

And now they're back together where they belong.

— Claire

How it works
Three types of material
Belief Your positions — what you think is true and why. Status moves from Exploring → Developing → Conviction. The spine everything else hangs from.
Proof Evidence. Stories, examples, patterns, data, perspectives. Six source types so you can see where the evidence is thin.
Craft How to express it. Format, visual, tone. Things you've noticed about how others deliver ideas well.
Two ways in
Think button
↓
For ideas that arrive messy. Dump what you've got — raw, unfiltered. Claude identifies the threads, spars with you until it resolves, then proposes a filing brief. You approve. Items land in the right places.
or
+ button
↓
For material ready to classify. Pick the function, fill in why you saved it. A suggestion engine proposes tags as you type.
Navigating

The Inbox holds things in progress — sparring sessions, half-filed ideas. Open one to continue the conversation or send it through.

Click any belief in the sidebar to pull up everything connected to it: proof that supports it, craft that would help you express it.

Filters at the top cut by function, domain, or proof source type. Search reaches inside notes and why-saved fields.
